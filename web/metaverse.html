The metaverse is real and here — even facebook changed their name to it. Because AR is a bridge between the digital and physical worlds (a phenomenon some are called the “phygital” or “metaverse”), tokenized AR videos and overlays can be applied to collectible goods such as sneakers, vinyl albums, and baseball cards—providing a unique experience for the collector and a significant boost in desirability and value. Linking digital assets to physical assets requires technology like Near Field Communication (NFC), which allows for communication between two nearby devices and can act, essentially, as electronic IDs or keycards.




A fan finds an old-school rap CD in his closet. With an NFT, he can authenticate and track ownership of his rap CD. Also, with augmented reality, he can bring his favorite music and artist to life. This rap CD is more special than other CDs because it’s a rare collectible. With an NFT integrated to the CD he can feel confident when selling, trading, or protecting his CD over time.




NFC tags contain a miniscule microchip that can be read via smartphone with just a tap—it’s the same technology that makes digital wallets like Apple Pay possible—so authenticity and ownership can be readily verified through the blockchain. Linking an AR experience to a collectible physical object through an NFC device ensures that the experience is unique to that particular object, adding value and investment potential. (If an owner ever sells the physical asset, the AR experience goes with it.)




An environmentalist and activist collects digital NFT animals, like the African penguin, beluga whale, and octopus. She can authenticate, track, sell and trade her animal NFTs to other people interested in promoting the mission/movement of improving the environment and the animals who live in it. As a value add, she can also bring the animals to life with augmented reality for a more valuable NFT and collector experience.
